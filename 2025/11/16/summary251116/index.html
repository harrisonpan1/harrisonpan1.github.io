Weekly Global Market Summary 11/16/2025

This week, the U.S. ended its record 43-day government shutdown. While the full impact remains to be seen (key economic data is still delayed), the relief initially boosted market sentiment. However, a rapid shift in Federal Reserve expectations led to a turbulent week across global markets.

U.S. Market: Shutdown Ends and Hawkish Fed

The end of the government shutdown initially lifted risk sentiment, causing U.S. stocks to rally early in the week. However, this momentum was quickly reversed when several Federal Reserve officials delivered hawkish speeches, causing the market’s expectation for a December rate cut to fall sharply. This brought downward pressure on stocks.

This combined with persisted concerns from the previous week about the over-valuation of AI stocks creates a highly volatile environment with technology stocks under pressure.

In the bond market, both the fading safe-haven demand after the shutdown ended and the reduction in rate cut expectations pushed Treasury yields higher. Although a weak ADP employment report caused a brief rally, U.S. Treasury prices ultimately fell (yields rose) for the week.

Europe & Japan: US Followor, But With Local Flavor

European stocks generally outperformed the U.S. The boost from the U.S. government shutdown ending provided warmth to the European market, but the shock of falling Fed rate cut expectations was less severe here. This meant European stocks tended to follow the U.S. up, but not down as much. European bond markets also tracked U.S. Treasuries, closing slightly lower.

The UK, however, dragged the average down. News broke that the UK Chancellor of the Exchequer had abandoned tax hike plans. The market interpreted this as a lack of commitment to medium- to long-term fiscal discipline, generating fiscal anxiety that simultaneously suppressed both the UK stock and bond markets.

Japanese bonds saw yields rise slightly, driven by U.S. Treasuries, but the increase was smaller, which widened the US-Japan interest rate differential, leading the yen to depreciate slightly. The lower rise in Japanese yields was partly due to the market pushing back its expectation for the Bank of Japan’s first rate hike from December to January.

Japanese stocks continued their correlation with U.S. tech stocks, but the depreciation of the yen provided a supporting tailwind, allowing the Japanese stock market to close with a small gain for the week.

Commodities: Gold Rallies, Oil Flat

The U.S. Dollar Index weakened slightly this week. Combined with the stock market’s persistent volatility over AI valuations and a renewed demand for safe-haven and inflation-hedge assets, gold prices ended the week up by about 2% after a period of sharp fluctuation.

A monthly report from OPEC raising concerns about a slowdown in medium- to long-term demand hit market confidence. However, supply disruptions caused by geopolitical conflicts provided support. After a tug-of-war between these factors, oil prices closed the week nearly flat.
