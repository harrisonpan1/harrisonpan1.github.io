Weekly Global Market Summary 12/14/2025

The central event this week was the Federal Reserve’s 25 basis point rate cut, lowering the target range to 3.5% to 3.75%, which fully met market expectations. However, the true market tension lies not in this cut itself, but in the path of future easing, leading to a subtle divergence between short-term market sentiment and long-term rate expectations.

U.S. Market: Small-Cap Rally, Tech Slump, and a Steeper Curve

The details of the Fed’s decision released cautious signals, tempering expectations for an accelerating pace of future cuts:

  • Dot Plot: The Dot Plot indicated that officials forecast possibly only one additional rate cut in 2026, and the vote showed increasing division.
  • Hawkish Commentary: Post-meeting remarks from hawkish officials expressed caution regarding further easing.

These factors combined to cause short-term rates to fall while long-term rates rose, resulting in a steepening yield curve . Notably, this scenario of a rate cut alongside rising long-term yields is not unique to this week but is a major trend characterizing the entire Fed easing cycle.

While strengthening rate cut expectations should theoretically push entire bond yields curve down (prices up), the “Goldilocks” environment (no immediate recession risk) meant safe-haven demand was weak. Optimistic sentiment drove funds from the bond market to the stock market, causing upward shift of U.S. Treasury yield curve (price to fall).

The stock market showed conflicting trends this week:

  • Positive Drivers: The rate cut decision itself, along with the Fed’s plan to purchase short-term Treasuries as needed, boosted sentiment, leading to a global rally on Thursday.
  • Negative Concerns: Disappointing earnings guidance from tech giants like Broadcom and Oracle reignited market anxiety over the AI valuation bubble.

The combination of these two factors resulted in a fragmented U.S. market: small-cap stocks rallied on the rate cut impulse, while tech stocks were weighed down and declined.

Europe & Japan: Tightening vs. Easing Expectations

The Europe market trend was similar to the U.S., but European bond prices fell more sharply than U.S. Treasuries. One reason is the renewed market speculation that the European Central Bank (ECB) could potentially pivot to hiking rates in 2026, a more hawkish stance than the Fed’s cautious easing.

The Bank of Japan (BOJ) continued its public communication campaign, paving the way for a likely rate hike decision next week.

  • Hike Expectations Solidify: By Friday, nearly all institutional economists forecast the BOJ would hike rates to 0.75% next week. Authoritative financial media like Nikkei also reported that the hike was virtually confirmed .
  • JGB Yields Climb: The expected return to tightening pushed government bond yields higher. This effect, combined with market concerns over increased bond issuance due to an anticipated expansion of the 2026 fiscal budget, caused Japanese government bond (JGB) yields to continue rising (prices to fall).
  • Nikkei Gain: Japanese stocks closed slightly higher, primarily benefiting from the momentum of the U.S. rally following the Fed cut. The decline in U.S. stocks on Friday did not affect the Nikkei’s closing price due to the time zone difference.

Commodities: Silver Outperforms, Oil Drops Sharply

The rate cut reduced the opportunity cost of holding gold, pushing gold prices higher. Silver, a high leveraged commodity with way smaller market size, not only benefited from gold’s momentum but also from the logic that lower rates would stimulate the economy and boost industrial demand (for more details on how silver market plays, please see this note). Consequently, silver surged more sharply than gold this week.

Oil prices dropped sharply this week. The primary driver was the assessment by the International Energy Agency (IEA) and other bodies that the global oil market faces a significant supply surplus in 2026, which overshadowed short-term supply disruptions.
