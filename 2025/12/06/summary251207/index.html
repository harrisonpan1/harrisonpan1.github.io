Weekly Global Market Summary 12/07/2025

As a new round of central bank meetings approaches, the main drivers of the global market this week once again focused on expectations for Federal Reserve rate cuts and Bank of Japan rate hikes. We’ll break down the logic region by region.

U.S. Market: Mixed Data and Raising Rate Cut Expectation

U.S. economic data released this week was mixed

  • Manufacturing PMI remained weak, but Services PMI rose.
  • ADP private sector employment turned negative in November, and corporate layoffs increased, yet initial jobless claims fell to a two-year low.
  • Relatively stable PCE inflation data.

Overall, economic data was slightly soft but showed no signs of a hard slowdown. Thus, market expectations for a Fed rate cut at next week’s meeting increase.

The environment of low recession risk coupled with rising rate cut expectations is generally beneficial for equities. Market sentiment leaned optimistic, driving capital into the stock market and pushing U.S. stocks slightly higher across the board.

The logic for U.S. Treasuries was subtle: while rate cut expectations typically push bond yields down, the absence of strong economic contraction risk meant safe-haven demand was weak. Optimistic sentiment drove funds from the bond market to the stock market, causing U.S. Treasury prices to decline (yields to rise).

Europe Market: Steady Pace and UK Anomaly

The data shows Eurozone core inflation is close to the 2% target, Q3 GDP growth was slightly revised up, and the unemployment rate held steady around 6.4%. The market believes the European Central Bank (ECB) has room for rate cuts next year but also recognizes that the easing cycle is nearing its end. Consequently, European stocks saw little change, and European bonds fell slightly, tracking the U.S. Treasury market.

The UK market remained an outlier: its bonds performed better than those in Germany and France, but its stock market lagged. The primary logic was that weak UK economic data intensified expectations for a BoE rate cut in December. This, combined with restored market confidence after the fiscal budget was announced a week prior, provided support for UK Gilts. However, restored market confidence also pushed the pound (GBP) stronger, indirectly applying downward pressure on the export-heavy UK stock market.

Japan Market: BOJ Hints Shock Global Bonds

Usually, Janpan bond markets follow the U.S., but the Japanese bond market shock on Monday visibly affected U.S. and European markets.

The Governor of the Bank of Japan (BOJ) stated in a speech on Monday that the economic and price targets were gradually being met. The market interpreted this as an intentional signal to prepare the public for a rate hike in December.

The 10-year Japanese Gilt yield surged to 1.95%, hitting a new high since 2007. The rate hike expectation also caused the yen to strengthen slightly against the dollar, which put some downward pressure on the Japanese stock market.

Commodities: Gold Consolidates, Oil Rises

The expectation of a Fed rate cut was one of the main forces pushing oil prices up.

  • why the expectation of a Federal Reserve rate cut push oil prices up?
    • The Fed rate cut can stimulate economic growth, which increase the global demand for energy
    • The Fed rate cut weakens the dollar, oil effectively becomes cheaper for buyers using non-dollar currencies
    • commodity like oil is a good hedge against the inflation

Additionally, OPEC’s suspended production increase plan and the volatile Ukraine-Russia situation provided further upward momentum. Oil prices closed the week slightly higher.

Gold benefited from the weaker dollar but was constrained by rising interest rates (global yields). After a sharp rally last week, gold entered a phase of high-level consolidation and pulled back slightly this week.
