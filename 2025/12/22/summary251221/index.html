Weekly Global Market Summary 12/14/2025

This past week has been a true “Super Central Bank Week” characterized by historic policy shifts and complex macro drivers. As the market grappled with multiple signals, the overarching narrative was one of resilience mixed with strategic caution.

Here is the deep-dive breakdown of the global market logic for this week.

U.S. Market: Inflation “Cooling” and the AI Earnings Rescue

The U.S. market navigated a “high-stakes oscillation” between weakening economic data and a high-profile recovery in tech sentiment.

  • “Cooling but not Crashing”: Recent economic data generally came in soft, yet investors interpreted this as a gradual slowdown rather than a sudden loss of momentum. Crucially, the November CPI (2.7% YoY) and Core CPI (2.6%) were lower than expected, reinforcing expectations for the Fed’s easing path.
  • Bond Market Rebound: The soft inflation print drove a broad decline in Treasury yields (e.g., the 10-year yield edged down to ~4.12%), resulting in a rally in U.S. government bond prices.
  • The AI Sentiment Rollercoaster:
    • The Dip: Midweek, technology stocks faced heavy selling pressure as concerns over AI “ROI” (Return on Investment) resurfaced. Anxiety was peaked by reports of financing delays for an Oracle-linked $10B data center project, causing a sharp tech-led drawdown on Wednesday.
    • The Rescue: The narrative flipped following Micron Technology’s (MU) blowout earnings and bullish guidance. Micron’s record Q1 results—with revenue jumping 57%—confirmed that AI hardware demand remains a powerful near-term profit driver. This “flight to quality” helped the Nasdaq and S&P 500 snap losing streaks and recover through Friday.

Europe: Steady Resilience and “Slow Easing” Realism

European markets showed greater stability compared to the AI-driven volatility of Wall Street.

The European Central Bank maintained its policy rates while upwardly revising growth forecasts. Market participants perceive the ECB as being in a relatively comfortable position, remaining data-dependent rather than signaling an aggressive pivot.

While the Bank of England cut rates, the 5-4 vote and cautious phrasing tempered expectations for a rapid easing cycle. This led to a general rise in European bond yields as the market priced in a slower-than-hoped-for cut schedule.

With a lower concentration of AI-sensitive tech giants, European equities outperformed U.S. indices this week, buoyed by improving regional economic outlooks and stabilizing inflation.

Japan: A Symbolic “30-Year High” and the Yen’s Inverse Move

The Bank of Japan (BOJ) made headlines by pushing rates to levels not seen since the mid-1990s.

On Friday, the BOJ raised its policy rate to 0.75%, a significant 30-year peak. However, because this move was heavily telegraphed by the media over the previous two weeks, the decision was “fully priced in.”

In a seemingly counterintuitive move, the Yen weakened against the Dollar after the hike. Governor Kazuo Ueda’s post-meeting press conference was characteristically vague regarding future hikes, leading the market to interpret his tone as “dovish.” Nikkei futures surged as a result.

Despite the Yen’s weakness, the hike confirmed higher long-term financing costs. Combined with concerns over Japan’s 2026 fiscal expansion and increased bond supply, the 10-year JGB yield broke through the 2.0% psychological barrier.

Commodities: Precious Metals Shine as Oil Slips

Gold Hits New Records. Lower U.S. Treasury yields reduced the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets, pushing Gold futures to new all-time highs.

Crude oil faced a “demand vs. supply” squeeze:

  • Supply: Speculation surrounding Russia-Ukraine peace talks eased some geopolitical supply fears.
  • Demand: News of slowing industrial and retail growth in China dampened demand expectations. Despite late-week support from U.S.-Venezuela tensions, oil prices ended slightly lower for the week.
