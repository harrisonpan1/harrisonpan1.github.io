Weekly Global Market Summary 01/11/2026

The year 2026 began with a “black swan” event: on January 3rd, the Trump administration launched a surprise operation involving the Venezuelan presidency. While such geopolitical escalation typically triggers a “flight to safety,” global equity markets, however, responded with a comprehensive rally. With economic data lacking a clear direction this week, the market’s performance was driven almost entirely by a shift in sentiment that defied conventional intuition.

U.S.: Geopolitics, Energy Giants, and AI Heat

The Venezuelan crisis initially saw gold and oil prices jump at the opening bell. However, as the initial shock faded, investors shifted to a long-term strategic perspective:

  • The Geopolitical Logic: While the intervention was aggressive, Trump’s rhetoric centered on “unlocking” Venezuela’s vast oil reserves through lifted sanctions and increased investment. This created a dual impact: a long-term expectation of increased global oil supply (putting downward pressure on prices) and a massive potential opportunity for U.S. energy giants to enter the Venezuelan market. Consequently, the energy sector led the charge upward.
  • AI and Tech Momentum: Beyond geopolitics, the tech sector remained white-hot. Strong performance from TSMC and the excitement surrounding the CES exhibition continued to fuel AI investment enthusiasm.
  • New Records: Combined with a rotation into defense sectors due to geopolitical risks and a reversal in U.S. housing policy sentiment, major U.S. indices once again shattered all-time highs.
  • Flatten Yield Curve: U.S. Treasuries fluctuated amid mixed economic data and geopolitical noise, eventually ending the week with a slightly flatter yield curve.

Europe: Mirror of US

The European markets largely mirrored the U.S. sentiment-driven rally. Stock market was propelled by the same optimism seen in the U.S., with energy and defense sectors outperforming. And the government bonds actually performed stronger than U.S. Treasuries, primarily supported by regional data showing a steady cooling of inflation.

Japan: Political Rumors and the Yen’s Decline

The Japanese market opened its first full trading week of the year by catching up to global volatility while contending with significant domestic political shifts.

Rumors surfaced on Friday suggesting that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, currently enjoying a high approval rating of around 70%, may dissolve the House of Representatives for a snap election as early as February. This prospect of political stabilization initially excited equity markets, with the Nikkei 225 jumping nearly 3% to reach fresh record highs.

However, the move is a double-edged sword: the market anticipates that a stronger mandate for Takaichi would pave the way for her “Sanaenomics” agenda of aggressive, proactive fiscal expansion. This alarm over fiscal sustainability triggered a sharp depreciation of the Yen, which in turn boosted export-oriented stocks but pushed Japanese Government Bond (JGB) yields to 27-year highs near 2.14% as investors worried about the inflationary impact and potential further rate hikes by the Bank of Japan.

Commodities: A Tug-of-War Between Risk and Supply

Oil price saw a “spike and fade” pattern. The geopolitical risk premium was eventually offset by the prospect of Venezuela’s reserves returning to the global market under U.S. influence.

Gold remained supported by the underlying geopolitical tension, though the broader “risk-on” mood in equities limited its upward momentum.
